Mission Statement & Origin Story

Why GravesMail exists and the path that led to building it

Our Mission

GravesMail exists for one reason: to put you back in control of your own inbox.

Every day, millions of people open their email and find it filled with messages they never asked for. Some are harmless ads. Others are dangerous. They pretend to be your bank, your employer, or a government agency. They try to trick you into clicking a link, giving up a password, or handing over your money. These aren’t just annoying — they’re crimes. And right now, almost nobody is stopping them.

The big email providers have decided this is acceptable. They scan your messages, build profiles about you, and sell that information to advertisers. They let spam and phishing attacks pour into your inbox because filtering them out isn’t what makes them money. You are what makes them money.

GravesMail takes a different approach. Your email is yours. We don’t read it. We don’t scan it for ad targeting. We don’t build a profile about you. We don’t sell anything about you to anyone. Ever.

Instead, we built a powerful spam and threat detection system that runs entirely on your own computer. It catches dangerous messages before you ever see them. It learns and gets smarter over time. And when users choose to share their anonymous detection data with us, every copy of GravesMail in the world gets better at protecting everyone.

Our goal isn’t just to protect your inbox. It’s to make spam and phishing so expensive and so unsuccessful that the people behind it can’t afford to keep doing it. The more people who use GravesMail, the harder it gets for bad actors to reach anyone. They don’t stop because someone asks them to. They stop because they run out of money.

How GravesMail Started

GravesMail was never supposed to exist. It was born out of frustration, after every other option had been tried and failed.

First, I Asked the Email Providers for Help

It started the way most people would start. I contacted the major email providers — the companies whose platforms carry billions of messages every day — and asked them to do more. My inbox was being flooded with spam, and much of it wasn’t just unwanted marketing. It was malicious. Phishing attacks. Fraud attempts. Messages that were flat-out illegal for the sender to have sent in the first place.

I asked the providers to step up and protect their users. Not a single one responded. Not one.

Then, I Went to the Authorities

I contacted law enforcement and cybercrime agencies at every level I could reach — local, county, city, state, federal, and international. I didn’t just file vague complaints. I did the investigative work myself. I traced the bad actors to specific addresses, specific buildings, specific people. I found their names, their phone numbers, their social media profiles. I collected forensic data and laid out exactly what they were doing, step by step, in detail that would hold up as digital evidence.

Almost nobody responded. The few who did took no action. The evidence sat there, untouched.

Then, I Went After the Bad Actors Myself

I scanned their infrastructure. I traced their IP addresses and mapped their operations across data centers and cloud services. When they moved, I followed. I chased them from one hosting provider to another. But that’s the problem with playing defense against people who have nothing to lose — they just keep moving.

Finally, I Realized the Only Answer Was to Build Something

The providers won’t help because it doesn’t make them money. Law enforcement can’t or won’t act because the problem crosses too many borders. Confronting the criminals directly is an endless game of whack-a-mole. None of these approaches solve the problem.

So I sat down and asked a different question: What if the technology itself made spam and phishing obsolete?

Spam exists because it’s cheap to send and enough people fall for it to turn a profit. If you can break that equation — if the messages never get seen, never get clicked, and never generate a single cent of revenue — the economics collapse. The criminals don’t stop because someone asked them to. They stop because they can no longer afford to operate.

That’s the idea behind GravesMail. Every installation makes the network smarter. Every user who opts in to share anonymous data strengthens the filter for everyone else. The more GravesMail spreads, the more expensive it becomes for bad actors to succeed.

They die by cost.

What We Believe

Your email is a private conversation, not a product to be harvested.
The companies that carry your messages have a duty to protect them, and they have failed that duty.
The best way to stop criminals isn’t to chase them. It’s to take away their income.
Technology should serve the people who use it — not the people who want to exploit them.
GravesMail was built by someone who got tired of waiting for someone else to fix the problem. If you’re tired of waiting too, you’re in the right place.
